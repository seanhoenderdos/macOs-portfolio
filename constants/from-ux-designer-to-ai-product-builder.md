# From UX Designer to AI Product Builder: My Journey into Full-Stack Product Building

I have always been someone who tries to optimize things.

That has shown up in different ways over time: how I learn, how I work, how I think about systems, and how I approach product building. I am naturally curious about making things clearer, faster, more useful, and more effective.

That curiosity is what pulled me into tech.

My path did not begin with a traditional computer science background. I was studying marketing, and I graduated summa cum laude. At the time, I wanted to break into tech, so I started learning UX design and coding. What began as a way to enter the industry became something much bigger: a growing interest in how products are imagined, designed, built, tested, and improved.

Since December 2021, AI has been one of the biggest areas of curiosity for me. I did not start exploring it because it was a trend I wanted to attach myself to. I followed it because it felt like a major shift in how software could be built and how people could work.

That curiosity has led me from marketing to UX, from UX to frontend development, and from frontend development into full-stack AI product building.

## Marketing Taught Me to Think About People

My marketing background still influences how I build.

Marketing taught me to care about the audience, the message, the positioning, and the reason someone should care. It taught me that a product is not just a collection of features. It has to connect with a real person trying to solve a real problem.

That way of thinking became useful when I moved into UX design.

In UX, I could take the same interest in people and apply it to interfaces, flows, and product decisions. I started thinking more deeply about how users move through a product, where they get stuck, what information they need, and how design can reduce friction.

That became one of the foundations of how I work now. I do not want to build technology for its own sake. I want to understand who it is for and what job it needs to do.

## UX Gave Me Product Taste

UX design helped me develop product taste.

By product taste, I mean the ability to look at a product and sense whether it is clear, useful, trustworthy, and well-structured. It is the ability to ask whether a user would understand what to do next, whether the interface supports the workflow, and whether the product feels aligned with the problem it is solving.

That has been important as I have moved deeper into engineering.

Code can make something work, but design judgment helps decide what should be built in the first place. When both are present, the product becomes stronger. I can think through the experience and then move into implementation without losing the user's perspective.

That combination is one of the things I value most about my path. I am not only interested in making interfaces look good. I am interested in making products work well for the people who use them.

## Learning to Code Changed What I Could Build

Learning to code changed my relationship with ideas.

Before I could build, an idea had to stay in a document, a wireframe, or a prototype. Once I started developing, I could turn ideas into working products. That shift was powerful.

I began building with modern JavaScript tools, including React, Next.js, TypeScript, React Native, and supporting tools like Prisma, Supabase, Tailwind CSS, and Vercel. Over time, I became more comfortable moving across the stack: designing interfaces, building frontend experiences, connecting data, working with authentication, integrating APIs, and shaping product flows.

The more I built, the more I realized that I enjoy the full product process. I like moving from a rough idea to a working system. I like figuring out the user problem, designing the workflow, building the interface, connecting the logic, and improving the product through feedback.

That is why I do not see myself as only one narrow title. The roles I am drawn to sit at the intersection of UX, full-stack development, AI, and automation.

## AI Became a Natural Extension of My Curiosity

AI became interesting to me because it changes what is possible for builders.

It can help turn rough ideas into product briefs. It can assist with research, interface exploration, code generation, refactoring, debugging, review, and automation. But the part that interests me most is not just speed. It is how AI can be designed into products in a way that helps people work better.

That is one of the reasons I built Lighted.

Lighted is an AI-powered sermon preparation workspace for pastors and Bible teachers. It helps users move from scattered ideas to structured, research-backed sermon material. Users can record audio in the app or send a Telegram voice note, have it transcribed with Whisper, and receive an exegesis brief with sections like transcript, word studies, historical context, and outline points.

What made Lighted meaningful as a product challenge was the ethical boundary. Pastors did not want AI to replace their own preparation, conviction, or voice. They wanted support without sacrificing integrity or prayer life.

That taught me an important lesson: the best AI products support human judgment instead of replacing it.

## Building for Real Business Needs

Another project that shaped my growth is Shearon Pharmaceutical.

ShearonPharma is a pharmaceutical wholesale website designed for doctors, pharmacists, and healthcare professionals. I designed and developed the site, using Next.js, GSAP, and Tailwind CSS to create a fast, polished, responsive experience.

The project required more than visual design. I had to understand the target audience, the business context, and what information mattered most. Research showed that BEE status was important for government contracts, while product indications and medical clarity mattered most to doctors and pharmacists.

That shaped the interface and content structure. I focused on clear calls to action, simple navigation, trustworthy visuals, and a professional experience that made it easier for healthcare professionals to request stock and find medical information.

I also designed the Shearon logo, created packaging concepts in Figma, and produced mockups in Adobe. Because the deadline was tight, I moved quickly into prototyping rather than designing every screen in Figma first.

That project reflects a part of my process I care about: adapting to the needs of the work. Sometimes the right approach is a full design phase. Sometimes the right approach is fast prototyping, strong judgment, and continuous refinement.

## The Thread Through My Work

Looking back, the thread through my journey is not one specific tool or job title.

It is the ability to connect disciplines.

Marketing helps me think about positioning and audience. UX helps me understand workflows and user needs. Engineering helps me turn ideas into working products. AI helps me move faster, explore more possibilities, and build new kinds of tools. Automation helps me reduce repeated manual work and create cleaner systems.

Together, those skills shape how I approach product building.

I am adaptable because I have had to learn across disciplines. I learn quickly because curiosity keeps pulling me into the next layer. I care about product taste because I started with users and design. I have technical drive because I want to build the thing, not only describe it.

That mix is where I feel strongest.

## Where I Am Building Toward

Right now, I am focused on building practical AI and full-stack products with strong user experiences.

I am interested in roles where I can combine UX thinking, frontend quality, full-stack development, AI workflows, and automation. I want to work on products that are useful, thoughtful, and built with care.

My journey into tech has been driven by curiosity, but it has also become more intentional over time. I am not just learning tools. I am learning how to bring product thinking and engineering together.

That is the kind of builder I am becoming: someone who can understand the user, shape the product, design the experience, build the system, and use AI responsibly to move faster without losing judgment.
